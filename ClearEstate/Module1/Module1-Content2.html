Probates

Probate With a Will : Testate 

When a person dies with a valid will (“testate”), Deceased’s estate will be distributed according to the terms of their will. In most cases, in order to do so, probate must first be granted. Probate is when a will is validated by the court. A probate is required to transfer certain types of assets such as real property and bank or investment accounts above a certain threshold.

Probate Without a Will: Intestate

When there is no will, someone needs to be appointed by the court to administer the estate, then called the personal representative. Anyone in the jurisdiction of domicile can apply but the court will usually follow the same rules for entitlement based on degree of relationship to the deceased, i.e., spouse, then child then. As long as anyone with a prior right (high in the order) agrees in writing, someone lower in the order can be appointed. If there is no one to apply, the Public Guardian & Trustee, or applicable local governing body may become involved. This process varies by jurisdiction, however, the average uncontested probate takes between 1 to 6 months once the application has been filed.

The purpose of statutes is to distribute the decedent's property in an organized and methodical way. Each jurisdiction has their own laws that determine how the property will be distributed.

Exploring Testates vs Intestates

With a will:

  • When a person dies with a valid will (“testate”), the Deceased’s estate will be distributed according to the terms of their will.
  • The will sets out the testator’s wishes including who they want to administer their estate (‘executor’), who their beneficiaries are and what each will receive
  • It should also include legal powers to facilitate the administration and it can often include funeral and/or burial wishes

Without a Will:

  • When someone dies without a will (“intestate”), the Court will distribute the estate among the heirs of the deceased, according to intestacy laws of the jurisdiction in which they were domiciled.
  • When there is no will, someone needs to be appointed by the court to administer the estate, then called the personal representative. Anyone in the jurisdiction of domicile can apply but the court will usually follow the same rules for entitlement based on the degree of relationship to the deceased, i.e., spouse, then child, then grandparents, then siblings
  • As long as anyone with a prior right (high in the order) agrees in writing, someone lower in the order can be appointed.
  • If there is no one to apply, the Public Guardian & Trustee may become involved.

Non-Probate Assets

Some of Decedent's assets may be titled in such a way that the assets are transferred by operation of law to the designated beneficiaries, or to the joint owner, upon Decedent’s death.

In those cases, additional paperwork will be necessary to complete the transfer, but not a full probate proceeding.

Examples of Non-Probate Assets

Examples of non-probate assets include: 

  • Property held in joint tenancy, or as community property with right of survivorship;
  • Property held in a trust;
  • Life insurance policy or annuity with a named beneficiary;
  • Retirement or pension account with a named beneficiary, such as an IRA or 401k account;
  • A brokerage account with a named beneficiary "Pay on Death" accounts;
  • Real Estate for which a valid “Revocable Transfer on Death Deed” had been recorded (n/a in Canada);
  • Canada Pension Plan Survivor’s Benefits; and
  • Veteran’s pensions.
